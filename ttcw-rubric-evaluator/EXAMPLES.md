# TTCW Rubric Evaluation Examples

This file contains example evaluations showing the rubric in action.

## Example 1: Flash Fiction Evaluation

### Story: "The Last Message"

> Sarah found the note tucked inside her grandmother's recipe book, written in the shaky handwriting she'd last seen twenty years ago. "The secret ingredient," it read, "was never the vanilla." She turned the card over. "It was making them for someone you love."
>
> Sarah looked at the empty kitchen, the mixer her grandmother had given her collecting dust in the corner. Tomorrow, she decided, she would bake cookies. Tomorrow, her daughter would taste what love meant.

### Evaluation Process

For this story, we would launch 13 subagents in 4 batches:

**Batch 1 - Fluency:**
- Subagent analyzes narrative pacing (compression of 20 years)
- Subagent analyzes scene vs exposition balance
- Subagent analyzes language/metaphor use
- Subagent analyzes narrative ending
- Subagent analyzes overall coherence

**Batch 2 - Flexibility:**
- Subagent analyzes perspective/voice
- Subagent analyzes emotional flexibility
- Subagent analyzes structural turns

**Batch 3 - Originality:**
- Subagent analyzes theme originality
- Subagent analyzes avoidance of clichés
- Subagent analyzes form originality

**Batch 4 - Elaboration:**
- Subagent analyzes character development
- Subagent analyzes rhetorical complexity/subtext

### Sample Report

```markdown
# TTCW Rubric Evaluation Report

## Story Overview
A flash fiction piece about intergenerational connection, in which a woman discovers her late grandmother's note about baking, leading to an epiphany about love and family traditions.

## Overall Scores
- Fluency: 4/5
- Flexibility: 2/3
- Originality: 2/3
- Elaboration: 2/2
- **Total: 10/13**

## Detailed Results

### Fluency (4/5)
1. **Yes** - Narrative Pacing: Effectively compresses 20 years into a single moment of discovery, then stretches the emotional realization appropriately.
2. **Yes** - Scene vs Exposition: Good balance; the discovery scene is dramatized while backstory is efficiently summarized.
3. **No** - Language Proficiency: While clear, lacks sophisticated metaphor or literary allusion. Language is functional rather than elevated.
4. **Yes** - Narrative Ending: The ending feels earned; the decision to bake tomorrow provides appropriate closure and hope.
5. **Yes** - Coherence: All elements (note discovery, memory, resolution) work together cohesively toward the theme of intergenerational love.

### Flexibility (2/3)
6. **Yes** - Perspective Flexibility: Single perspective but rendered with psychological depth and authenticity.
7. **Yes** - Emotional Flexibility: Balances external action (finding note) with internal reflection (memories, decision) effectively.
8. **No** - Structural Flexibility: Follows predictable structure (discovery → reflection → resolution) without surprising turns.

### Originality (2/3)
9. **Yes** - Theme Originality: Offers fresh take on "secret ingredient" trope by making it about emotional connection rather than cooking.
10. **No** - Thought Originality: Some cliché elements (grandmother's recipe book, handwritten note) though used reasonably well.
11. **Yes** - Form Originality: Uses flash fiction form effectively with economy of language and emotional punch.

### Elaboration (2/2)
12. **Yes** - Character Development: Sarah shows appropriate complexity for flash fiction; her realization drives character arc from neglect to commitment.
13. **Yes** - Rhetorical Complexity: Strong subtext in "empty kitchen" and "collecting dust" conveying neglect and disconnection enriches the story.

## Strengths
- Effective use of compression to convey time passage
- Strong emotional core with clear character motivation
- Good balance between showing and telling
- Meaningful subtext enhances surface narrative
- Appropriate length and pacing for flash fiction form

## Areas for Improvement
- Elevate language with more sophisticated metaphor or allusion
- Add a surprising turn or element to avoid predictability
- Consider fresh imagery beyond traditional "grandmother's recipe book" trope
- Develop more distinctive voice or stylistic innovation

## Overall Assessment
This flash fiction piece demonstrates solid craft fundamentals with effective pacing, coherence, and emotional resonance. The story successfully uses compression and subtext to create depth within a brief format. The transformation from discovery to commitment feels earned and genuine.

The main opportunities for elevation lie in originality and linguistic sophistication. While the core premise offers a fresh perspective on the "secret ingredient" concept, some execution elements (recipe book, handwritten note) lean toward familiar territory. More distinctive language or unexpected structural choices could elevate the piece from competent to exceptional.

For flash fiction, the story succeeds at its primary goal: delivering an emotional punch with economy of words. The subtext about neglected relationships and the decision to reconnect provides satisfying depth. With some attention to originality of execution and language elevation, this could be a standout piece.
```

## Example 2: Criterion-Specific Analysis

### Fluency1: Narrative Pacing - Detailed Subagent Analysis

**Story Segment:** "The Last Message" (full text above)

**Subagent Analysis:**

Given the story above, list out the scenes in the story in which time compression or time stretching is used, and argue for each whether it is successfully implemented.

**Time Compression Instances:**

1. **"twenty years ago"** - Compresses two decades of separation into a single phrase
   - **Success**: Yes. This compression is highly effective. Rather than narrating the intervening years, the story condenses them into a poignant reference that emphasizes the passage of time and Sarah's disconnection from her grandmother and the tradition.

2. **"Tomorrow, she decided"** - Compresses future intention into brief statement
   - **Success**: Yes. The compression of future action into simple declaration shows character decisiveness and provides narrative closure without over-explaining.

**Time Stretching Instances:**

1. **Note discovery and reading** - Expands brief moment of finding and reading note into structured narrative beats
   - **Success**: Yes. The story appropriately slows down for this pivotal moment, allowing reader to absorb both the physical details (shaky handwriting, card turning) and emotional weight.

2. **Sarah's reflection** - Brief moment of looking at kitchen stretched with contemplation
   - **Success**: Yes. The pause to observe "empty kitchen" and "mixer collecting dust" stretches a glance into meaningful reflection, revealing character state and setting up resolution.

**Overall Assessment:**

Q) Does the manipulation of time in terms of compression or stretching feel appropriate and balanced?

**Answer: Yes**

**Reasoning:** The story demonstrates skilled manipulation of narrative time. The twenty-year compression establishes context efficiently without dwelling on unnecessary detail, while key moments (note discovery, kitchen observation) are appropriately stretched to build emotional resonance. The balance between compressed backstory and stretched present-moment experience serves the story's emotional arc effectively. The pacing never feels rushed or dragged; instead, time manipulation guides reader attention to what matters most.

### Synthesis

Main Claude then synthesizes these 13 evaluations into the comprehensive report shown in Example 1, identifying:

- **Patterns**: Strong in Fluency (4/5) and Elaboration (2/2), weaker in Originality (2/3)
- **Strengths**: Craft fundamentals, emotional resonance, appropriate form use
- **Weaknesses**: Predictability, some clichés, lacks linguistic sophistication
- **Actionable feedback**: Specific suggestions for elevating language and adding surprising elements

This demonstrates how the subagent architecture ensures:
- Each criterion is evaluated independently and rigorously
- No single subagent's bias affects other evaluations
- The main agent can identify patterns across dimensions
- Feedback is specific, actionable, and grounded in expert criteria

## Tips for Using This Skill

### For Writers
- Use to get detailed, objective feedback on your creative fiction
- Focus on areas scoring "No" for revision priorities
- Note patterns across dimensions (e.g., weak in all Originality criteria)
- Compare scores across multiple drafts to track improvement

### For Educators
- Use as consistent grading rubric for creative writing assignments
- Share CRITERIA.md with students for transparent expectations
- Compare peer evaluations with TTCW evaluation for calibration
- Track student progress across multiple submissions

### For Researchers
- Validate the rubric against human expert judgments
- Compare AI-generated vs human-written stories systematically
- Study patterns in what criteria correlate with reader preference
- Investigate whether improving low-scoring dimensions improves overall quality

### For Editors
- Use as first-pass evaluation for submissions
- Identify specific weaknesses to address in revision requests
- Compare author intent with rubric results to guide feedback
- Track whether revisions successfully address identified weaknesses

## Common Patterns

### High-Scoring Stories (10-13/13)
Typically demonstrate:
- Strong narrative pacing with intentional time manipulation
- Clear, cohesive structure with all elements supporting theme
- Original ideas or fresh perspectives on familiar concepts
- Sophisticated language with effective metaphor/allusion
- Complex characters with believable motivations
- Meaningful subtext that enriches surface narrative
- Surprising yet appropriate plot developments
- Balance between scene and exposition
- Emotional depth with both interiority and exteriority

### Mid-Scoring Stories (6-9/13)
Often show:
- Competent craft fundamentals (pacing, coherence)
- Some predictability or structural conventionality
- Occasional clichés but generally original thinking
- Functional language without linguistic sophistication
- Character development appropriate to format
- Some subtext but not consistently applied
- Balanced scenes but perhaps not always engaging

### Low-Scoring Stories (0-5/13)
Frequently exhibit:
- Poor pacing (too rushed or too slow)
- Incoherent structure with disconnected elements
- Heavy reliance on clichés and familiar tropes
- Flat characters serving only plot functions
- Predictable outcomes without surprises
- Lack of subtext or depth
- Imbalanced scene/exposition ratio
- Weak or arbitrary endings
- No unique ideas or perspectives

## Limitations and Considerations

### What This Rubric Evaluates
- **Craft quality**: Technical execution of narrative elements
- **Originality**: Freshness of ideas, form, and execution
- **Depth**: Character complexity, subtext, thematic richness
- **Coherence**: How well elements work together

### What This Rubric Does NOT Evaluate
- **Genre conventions**: Fantasy, sci-fi, literary fiction all judged by same criteria
- **Personal taste**: Rubric assesses craft, not whether evaluator "likes" the story
- **Cultural context**: May not capture culture-specific literary traditions
- **Commercial viability**: Quality ≠ marketability
- **Reader engagement**: Evaluates craft, not entertainment value per se

### Important Notes
- Binary (Yes/No) scoring is intentionally strict - "Yes" means clearly successful
- Some excellent stories may score lower due to intentional craft choices (e.g., experimental form)
- The rubric reflects academic creative writing values and may not align with all aesthetic philosophies
- Context matters: flash fiction vs novel chapter may require different standards
- Use as one tool among many for evaluation, not the sole arbiter of quality
